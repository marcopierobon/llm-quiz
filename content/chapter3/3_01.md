You've learned the fundamentals. Now it's time to do it for real.


This week, you'll take a base model, fine-tune it on a real dataset, and watch it get measurably better at a specific task.


This isn't a toy example. You'll use the same tools, workflows, and infrastructure that production ML teams rely on—starting simple in Colab, then scaling to cloud GPUs with reproducible, config-driven pipelines.


By week's end, you'll have fine-tuned your first model, tracked your experiments properly, and understood exactly how to move from notebook prototyping to scalable training runs.

What You'll Accomplish This Week
In Weeks 1 and 2, you built your foundation—architectures, benchmarks, tokenization, LoRA, quantization, and how LLMs actually learn.

Now you’ll put it all together.

This week tells one simple story: take a base model, make it better, and prove it.

You’ll start by evaluating a base model on a summarization dataset called Samsum, then fine-tune it using different methods, compare ROUGE scores, and scale your workflow to professional-grade cloud GPUs.

We’ll also look briefly at what happened when we tried fine-tuning on GSM8K, a math reasoning dataset, and why that task required more than simple supervised fine-tuning.

By the end, you’ll have a complete, reproducible fine-tuning pipeline—one you can reuse for any model or task.

The Task: Teaching a Model to Summarize Conversations
This week, we’ll use SAMSum — a dataset of short messenger-like dialogues paired with human-written summaries.

It’s perfect for fine-tuning because it’s:

Realistic: it mirrors how people actually converse in chat or customer support scenarios.
Compact: small enough for fast fine-tuning runs, but large enough to show improvement.
Evaluated with ROUGE: a standard summarization metric that measures overlap between model and reference summaries.
You’ll start with a base model like Llama 3.2 1B, evaluate its baseline summarization quality, and then fine-tune it to produce more concise, relevant summaries.

By the end, you’ll have a model that’s quantifiably better — not just in how it reads, but in measurable ROUGE scores.

🎥 Week 3 Mindset: Preparing for the Hard Part
This video prepares you for Week 3 and beyond — the point where you move from theory to hands-on LLM fine-tuning. You'll learn what makes this work challenging, why establishing baselines matters, and how to develop the troubleshooting skills that separate real engineers from tutorial followers.


Your Week 3 Roadmap
Lesson 1 – SAMSum Fine-Tuning Project Establishing Your Baseline Performance

Lesson 2 – Fine-Tuning Frontier LLMs (OpenAI) Try managed fine-tuning through OpenAI’s API to see a fast, hosted workflow.

Lesson 3 – Complete QLoRA Training Pipeline (SAMSum) Fine-tune your open-weight model end-to-end on Samsum using QLoRA, monitoring training loss and ROUGE improvements.

Lesson 4 – RunPod Intro Set up RunPod cloud GPUs to train faster with fewer resource limits.

Lesson 5 – Experiment Tracking & Reproducibility (W&B) Integrate Weights & Biases to log metrics and artifacts for reproducibility.

Lesson 6 – RunPod End-to-End Fine-Tuning and Experimentation Run your full fine-tuning pipeline remotely using configs and experiment tracking.

Three Key Shifts This Week
From theory to practice.
You’ll go from learning about fine-tuning to actually doing it—seeing real performance improvements.

From notebooks to infrastructure.
You’ll move from Colab to cloud GPUs and config-driven pipelines, mirroring real production workflows.

From “it works” to “it’s reproducible.”
You’ll track experiments, version results, and document your process—the habits of professional ML engineers.

A Quick Note on Pacing
This is the most hands-on week yet. Expect bugs, dependency conflicts, and GPU memory errors—they’re part of the job.

You might face:

CUDA out-of-memory issues
Library version mismatches
YAML misconfigurations
RunPod setup quirks or authentication hiccups
That’s normal. Troubleshooting is core to LLM engineering. Each fix deepens your understanding.

When something breaks, slow down, read the error carefully, and use it as a guide. Check docs, GitHub issues, and experiment with parameters. You’ll start to recognize patterns—and that’s when the work starts feeling natural.

Your Next Step
In Lesson 1, you’ll establish your baseline: load Llama 3.2 1B, evaluate it on SAMSum, and record its baseline ROUGE performance.

That’s your starting point. The rest of the week is about making those summaries sharper, shorter, and more accurate.

Let’s begin.
